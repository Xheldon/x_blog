Data-Driven Life (DDL: Data-Driven Life)

✍🏼 Written on Jun 25, 2026    💡 Updated on Jun 27, 2026

I. The Unrecorded Two Kilometers

Last weekend, I went for a walk in Olympic Forest Park. After about twenty minutes, I habitually glanced at my wrist to check how long I’d been walking. Normally, after around 10 minutes of walking, the Apple Watch detects the activity and prompts you to start a workout. If you confirm, it counts the past 10 minutes as part of your exercise time.

But occasionally—like today—when I raised my wrist, I realized the workout hadn’t been recorded at all. The rings were stuck at zero, as if that entire stretch of walking never existed in the data world. In that moment, what washed over me wasn’t the healthy frustration of “I put in effort for nothing,” but a more absurd sense of weightlessness—that walk didn’t count. I even half-seriously considered turning around and starting over.

I’ll admit it: a significant part of why I exercise is to produce a record that says, “I worked out.” Exercise is the means; the data is the end goal. It sounds backwards, but if you also use a smartwatch or a fitness app, you probably know exactly what I mean—you’re not alone.

2. Naming This Condition: DDL

I’ve named this mindset DDL: Data-Driven Life. (Yes, it’s a programmer’s dad joke, punning on “deadline”—in a way, it is indeed a kind of deadline, an implicit “today’s grid must be filled” pressure.)

Once you give it a name, you realize it’s no longer confined to fitness—it has seeped into every crevice of life:

  • GitHub’s green grid. Just to make the contribution chart look good, people deliberately find things to commit. Even crazier, some sell services on to generate fake code commits for the chart.

  • Duolingo’s streak counter. Sometimes you open the app not to learn, but to avoid resetting that consecutive-day counter—and then there’s the “friend streak” pressure, which is another story.

  • Douban’s “Read/Watched” tags. A book abandoned halfway, but the satisfaction from clicking “Mark as Read” can feel more real than finishing it. For example, I never had the energy to finish One Hundred Years of Solitude, but since I read the beginning, I still marked it as “read.”

  • Amap/Baidu Maps’ travel city unlocks. Countries and cities visited get “lit up” on the map, like passport stamps—“checked in N cities.”

  • WeChat step rankings, annual summaries. Even listening to music gets recorded, ranked, and summarized into a year-end report card.

At its core, DDL is a lifestyle: First ask, “Can this be logged?” then decide whether and how to do it. Recording shifts from a byproduct to a primary motive. I’m guilty of every single example above, often without a second thought—that’s what makes it so insidious. It doesn’t feel like a flaw; it feels like a habit.

3. There Are Two Versions of Me: One for Social Media, One Just for Myself

At this point, we need to make a distinction—otherwise, things will get increasingly confusing. Behind DDL, there are actually two “selves” with different motivations.

The first self is the signaling one. Posting cycling route maps or calorie counts from hikes on social media is, frankly, about looking cool and enjoying the thrill of showing off. It’s a social signal—broadcasting to the world: “I’m disciplined, I’m healthy, I have the time and money to travel abroad for fun.” When economist Veblen talked about “conspicuous consumption,” today we might as well call it conspicuous fitness. This part of the motivation is undeniably tied to vanity.

The second self is the private recorder. Some data I never share; I just revisit it alone, over and over. A solo cycling route, a hike I didn’t tell anyone about, an unexpected food discovery—no audience, yet I still record it, save it, and occasionally revisit it. This part has almost nothing to do with vanity. It’s more like a private ritual against forgetting, a ledger written only for myself (and my future self).

That sense of accomplishment after posting—does it come from the activity itself or the row of red hearts? My answer is: a mix, and not a particularly noble one. Sometimes, the likes are sweet, so sweet that I question why I even went out in the first place. But one detail gives me some comfort—the data I never share, the ones just for me, are precisely the ones I can’t bear to delete. They have no audience, yet they’re the ones I revisit the most. So I suspect the vanity part is real, but beneath it lies another half that has nothing to do with anyone else. Separating these two selves is crucial because when defending “vanity” later, we must first admit: a big part of DDL has nothing to do with vanity at all.

4. Digging Deeper: What Are We Really Chasing?

Vanity is merely the outermost layer. Dig a few layers deeper, and you’ll find that DDL taps into several more profound things.

First, instant feedback against delayed rewards. The true benefits of exercise—health, longevity, fitness—are slow, invisible variables. You won’t visibly see your muscles grow bigger after today’s run. But data forcibly compresses this feedback loop: you can’t see yourself getting healthier, but you can see the numbers rising. At its core, DDL is essentially slapping an “instant gratification” dashboard onto an activity that inherently requires “delayed gratification.” This mechanism, which leverages loss aversion (fear of breaking a streak, fear of an unclosed ring) to drive behavior, is practically welding behavioral economics directly into the watch.

Second, resisting entropy, resisting oblivion. Memory decays and deceives; delve a layer deeper, and it takes on an existential tone—an unrecorded experience carries a weightless sense of “it might as well not have happened” (remember those two kilometers at the beginning?). The fitness community’s blunt motto says it all: If it’s not on Strava, it didn’t happen. Every timestamped record is a letter to your future self, a clumsy negotiation with time: Don’t erase all that I’ve lived.

Third, a sense of control. Most of life is chaotic and uncontrollable. “Quantifiable” roughly equates to “legible,” and “legible” in turn equates to “controllable.” Slicing life into measurable metrics creates a sense of certainty amid the chaos—I’m making progress, I’m holding the wheel—even if that certainty is somewhat illusory.

Fourth, a commitment mechanism. This is essentially a switch with two sides. The darker side is when you’re midway through a workout and forget to start tracking, so you’d rather abandon it or start over—data ends up hijacking the exercise. But flip it around, and it’s precisely this “if it’s not recorded, it doesn’t count” obsession that forces you to lace up your shoes, step outside, and finish that session.

It’s a bit self-referential—I’m the one building these very tools: timestamped journals, iOS apps that aggregate fitness and health data. I complain about being data-driven while simultaneously crafting this dashboard for myself (and others). In a way, I’m both the prisoner and the architect of this panopticon.

V. In Defense of Vanity: Low-Quality Fuel, But It Does Ignite the Fire

Now we can directly answer that question: Is it legitimate to exercise out of vanity?

My view is: Vanity is inferior fuel, but it does get the fire going. And I don’t want to stop at the lukewarm judgment of “harmful but useful”—I’d like to push the argument further.

There’s a famous 18th-century notion called “private vices, public benefits” (Mandeville’s The Fable of the Bees)—that seemingly “base” motivations like vanity and greed actually drive “noble” outcomes like fashion, commerce, and ambition. Apply this to an individual: If a “low” motive (vanity) consistently produces a “high” result (health + discipline), is that motive really so shameful? A pragmatist would say, judge something by its fruits.

Going even further—knowing full well that you’re driven by vanity and deliberately leveraging it isn’t weakness; it’s self-engineering. Like Odysseus, who knew he couldn’t resist the sirens’ song, so he tied himself to the mast in advance. You don’t need to be a saint with pure motives; you can be the engineer of your own behavior: knowing you’re lazy, design a reward system that tricks even a sloth into moving. (Of course, this doesn’t mean claiming you can’t resist lust and thus scrolling TikTok daily to practice “edging”—stop right there, that’s another topic.)

That’s why I say, “Exercising for vanity, yet genuinely reaping the benefits, has intrinsic value”—the more I think about it, the more it holds up, and more convincingly than I initially assumed. I’ve lost count of the times I stepped out solely to close that damn activity ring, yet the wind, sweat, and soreness were real, and so was the grounded satisfaction afterward. Whether water is boiled by burning trash or nuclear fission, it’s still hot, and both can generate electricity.

VI. But Don’t Get Too Cocky: Do the Full Math

If the article ended at the previous section, it would merely be an elaborate self-defense. To be honest, we must also leave ample room for opposing views. The fuel of vanity comes with several real costs.

Goodhart’s Law: When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The danger lies in how activities are subtly distorted to make the metrics look better—for example, choosing a route with more impressive data but one you don’t truly want to take, or simply not moving at all once “this one doesn’t count.” At this point, the means have hijacked the ends.

The Overjustification Effect: External incentives can erode intrinsic enjoyment. This is a counterintuitive finding from self-determination theory. The worst outcome is this: the day the audience disappears or the data dries up, motivation collapses instantly, like influencers who stop working out when no one’s watching. You started running for yourself, but somewhere along the way, it became about running for others (a classic trope).

And the most subtle one: The loss of presence. Are you climbing the mountain, or are you “filming yourself climbing the mountain”? When experiences are mediated through lenses and sensors, they grow thinner. The person who keeps glancing at their pace while climbing isn’t fully present on that mountain. Here lies a trade-off, one I haven’t fully figured out myself: Deadlines might be trading “depth of presence” for “permanence of record.” The mindfulness tradition of “living in the moment” would call this a bad deal, but who’s to say that years later, a traceable route you can revisit won’t be more valuable than a hazy, half-remembered experience? This tension is something I’d rather lay bare than rush to resolve.

Of the three, the last one stings the most. There have been times when, while climbing, I’d obsess over whether my pace had dropped, if the trail would break, or if I’d forgotten to resume tracking after a break—only to realize, once it was over, that the mountain was already behind me, and I could barely recall what it actually looked like. In those moments, I’d wonder: Did I truly visit that mountain, or just its data?

7. A Self-Check Question: If My Watch Broke Forever, Would I Still Go?

After laying out both sides, I lean toward a compromise—one I consider more mature: vanity and data are best treated as training wheels, not the engine itself.

The value of training wheels lies in “getting started”—they trick you off the couch and over the hardest hurdle. Ideally, as you ride, intrinsic rewards gradually take over: the thrill of a stronger body, the flow state, the post-workout mental clarity. Once these kick in, data should fade into the background, becoming a nice-to-have rather than the sole motivator.

To gauge where you are, ask a simple but brutally honest question:

If my watch broke forever, would I still go?

If the answer is “Then I wouldn’t go,” you’re still reliant on training wheels—and there’s no shame in that. If it’s “I’d go, just bummed about missing the stats,” your engine is already running on its own. The goal is to graduate from extrinsic to intrinsic motivation; but there’s no disgrace in still needing scaffolding. You might never fully “graduate,” and that’s perfectly okay.

My own honest answer oscillates between the two. On good days, it’s “I’d go, just bummed about missing the stats”; on tired, unmotivated days, “Then I wouldn’t go” wins out. Could I ditch the watch? Probably not yet. But I refuse to feel ashamed—riding with training wheels is still better than leaving the bike untouched out of disdain for their “childishness.”

8. Closing: Impure persistence will always beat pure intentions

After circling around, I want to ground my conclusion somewhere deeper than “showing off looks cool.”

The highest form of DDL might not be vanity at all, but rather a quiet resistance against the inevitability of “I will eventually be forgotten.” Every timestamped record is a negotiation with time: “Don’t erase all of what I’ve lived.” Vanity is just the loudest, most superficial layer of this impulse.

As for whether the motivation is pure or not, my current answer is: don’t overthink it. A workout with impure motives that you actually stick to will always beat a pure intention that never leaves the couch. I stepped out the door to post on social media, but the wind truly touched my face, the sweat truly dripped, and the mountain was truly beneath my feet. That mountain won’t shrink an inch just because my reason for going out wasn’t noble.

So the next time I forget to hit record, I’ll probably still feel like giving up. But I hope that version of me can laugh it off and keep moving forward—because those two kilometers happened, whether they were recorded or not.

In the spirit of the Great Zhou Rites, I’d like to close with this:

I once heard that when craftsmen first learn their art, it’s mostly to boast about their skills in front of others;
when hunters first draw their bows, it’s mostly to hang their prey at the door for all to see.
But look—the craftsman, in all that boasting, actually hones their craft;
the hunter, in all that showing off, truly strengthens their arms.
Should their progress and strength be taken away just because their initial motives weren’t pure?
